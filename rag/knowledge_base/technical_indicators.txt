TITLE: Understanding Technical Indicators

Simple Moving Average (SMA) is the average closing price over a fixed window,
such as 20 or 50 days. It smooths out short-term noise to show the underlying
trend. When price trades above its SMA, the trend is generally considered
bullish for that timeframe; when below, bearish.

Exponential Moving Average (EMA) is similar to SMA but weights recent prices
more heavily, so it reacts faster to new information. EMA12 and EMA26 are
common short and medium-term trend measures.

Relative Strength Index (RSI) measures the speed and size of recent price
changes on a 0-100 scale. Traditionally, RSI above 70 is considered
"overbought" (the asset may be due for a pullback), and RSI below 30 is
considered "oversold" (the asset may be due for a bounce). RSI near 50
suggests no strong momentum in either direction. RSI is a momentum signal,
not a guarantee of reversal — assets can stay overbought or oversold for
extended periods during strong trends.

MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence) is the difference between the
12-day and 26-day EMA. A 9-day EMA of MACD itself is called the "signal
line." When MACD crosses above its signal line, it is often read as bullish
momentum building; crossing below is often read as bearish momentum
building. The MACD histogram (MACD minus signal line) shows the size of
that gap.

Bollinger Bands plot a moving average with bands two standard deviations
above and below it. Price near the upper band suggests the asset is trading
at the high end of its recent volatility range; price near the lower band
suggests the low end. Bands widen during high volatility and narrow during
low volatility ("squeeze"), which some traders watch as a signal that a
larger move may be coming.

None of these indicators predict the future with certainty. They describe
what has already happened in price and volume data, and are best used
together rather than in isolation.
