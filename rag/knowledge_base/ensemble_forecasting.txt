TITLE: How FinSight AI's Ensemble Forecast Works

FinSight AI combines three separate models to produce its 7-day price
forecast: Linear Regression, ARIMA, and an LSTM neural network. Each model
sees the same historical closing prices but approaches the forecasting
problem differently.

Linear Regression fits a straight trend line through recent prices and
extrapolates it forward. It is simple and fast, and works reasonably well
when a stock is in a steady, gradual trend, but it cannot capture cycles,
reversals, or sudden changes in momentum.

ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) is a classical statistical
time-series model. It models a price series as a combination of its own past
values (autoregression) and past forecast errors (moving average), after
differencing the data to make it stationary. ARIMA can capture short-term
mean-reverting patterns that a simple trend line misses.

LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) is a type of recurrent neural network designed
to learn patterns across sequences of data. It is trained on a longer
history of price movements and can, in principle, learn more complex
non-linear patterns than the other two models. It is also the most
data-hungry and computationally expensive of the three, and its accuracy
depends heavily on how much clean historical data is available.

The final "ensemble" forecast is a weighted average of the three models'
predictions (roughly 50% Linear Regression, 30% ARIMA, 20% LSTM by default
in this app). If one model fails to produce a prediction for a given stock,
its weight is redistributed across the remaining models rather than
breaking the forecast entirely.

Important limitation: none of these models use information they weren't
trained on. They extrapolate from historical price patterns only — they do
not know about future news, earnings surprises, macroeconomic shocks, or
anything that hasn't already happened. A forecast is a statistical
projection, not a guarantee, and should not be treated as financial advice.
